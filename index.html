William Wyatt

PhD Economist studying how LLMs reason, decide, and take risks

I study the behavioral patterns that emerge when large language models are placed in economic decision-making contexts. My work uses game theory, causal inference, and large-scale experiments to measure how prompts shape AI behavior — with implications for deployment, alignment, and economic measurement.

Research

LLM behavioral alignment through the lens of economics

Job Market Paper

Participation or Observation: How Prompts Control LLM Reasoning

Empirical study across 283 prompt permutations identifying which key phrases cause LLMs to embody scenarios versus observe them — and how this shifts their strategic behavior.

Code

Under Review, 2026

The Role of Responsibility in AI's Strategic Risk-Taking

15,000+ observations showing LLMs are 73.2% more likely than humans to take risks when decisions impact others. Implications for AI deployment in economic decision-making.

Code

Working Paper

Behavioral Effects of Personas and Demographics

When LLMs play behavioral games, their behavior changes based on the persona they embody. Mapping the demographic dimensions that shift AI strategic choices.

Working Paper

Measurable Effects from Emergency Zero Bail in California

Quasi-experimental analysis of zero bail policy reform using causal inference methods to measure real-world outcomes of criminal justice policy interventions.
